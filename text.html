My Project

Self-driving car technology - The prospect of the automation industry in the future

Self-driving car technology is growing and becoming more prevalent as the market demands breakthroughs to usher in a new era for the automation industry.

History of self-driving car technology

- In 1939, during the General Motors show, Norman Bel Geddes created the first self-driving car. It was a radio-controlled electromagnetic vehicle made up of magnetized metal spikes mounted on the road.
- In 1977, Japan improved on this idea by using a camera system that transferred data to a computer to process images of the road.
However, this car can only move at a speed of about 32km / h. After a decade, the Germans continue to produce a vehicle equipped with a camera that can drive itself at a speed of about 90km / h.
As this technology improves, so does the ability of self-driving cars to detect and respond to the environment.
- From 1950 - 2000, safety/convenience features, cruise control seat belts and Antilock Brakes were explored.
- In the year 2000-2010, the features of automation, cruise control, blind spot detection, collision warning, lane departure and anti-lock braking continued to be developed. These features are intended to ensure the safety and convenience of users.
- From 2010 to 2016, driver assistance capabilities such as rearview camera, automatic emergency braking and lane-focused assist appeared. - Since 2016 until now, the features of autonomous vehicles have gradually become more autonomous such as: helping the driver stay in the right lane, developing ACC technology (Adaptive Cruise Control - adaptive cruise control system) and self-parking capabilities.

Working principle of self-driving car technology

To apply self-driving car technology, developers have integrated AI technology to provide power, combining large amounts of data from image recognition systems, machine learning and neural networks.
The neural network will identify patterns in the data fed to the machine learning algorithm.
This data is usually images captured on self-driving cars so that the neural network learns to identify traffic lights, curbs, pedestrians, trees, signs, and other parts of the driving environment. Cruise control system of self-driving car technology
The above technologies will combine with the features of autonomous vehicles available to operate such as:
- Adaptive Cruise Control (ACC): When stopping, it automatically maintains the distance between the driver's car and the vehicle in front.
- Lane-focused steering feature: when the car crosses the road markings, this feature will intervene by automatically pushing the car towards the opposite lane.
- Free steering wheel: this feature allows the operator to not need to put their hands on the steering wheel.
The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) classifies different levels of self-driving cars to determine how autonomous the driver and vehicle are when operating.
Specifically, there are six levels of self-driving cars from 0 to 5 and each level will represent different tasks and roles.
